Motor Selection

Guide to choosing the right motors for your FTC robot based on speed, torque, and application needs.

Understanding Motor Specifications

RPM (Revolutions Per Minute)

How fast the motor spins. Higher RPM = faster movement. Typical FTC motors range 30-312 RPM at stall.

Torque

Rotational force measured in oz-in. Higher torque = more pushing power and ability to lift heavy loads.

Stall Torque

Maximum torque when motor is completely blocked. Used for calculating lifting capacity.

Power

Measured in watts. Affects heat output and energy consumption.

Common FTC Motors

REV HD Hex Motor

Specifications: 312 RPM, 76 oz-in stall torque

Best For: Drivetrain, mechanisms requiring high torque

Cost: Medium

Notes: Most popular choice, reliable, good balance of speed and power

REV Core Hex Motor

Specifications: 312 RPM, 64 oz-in stall torque

Best For: Budget builds, secondary motors

Cost: Lower

Notes: Lighter and cheaper than HD Hex, slightly lower torque

gobilda Yellow Jacket Motor

Specifications: Multiple ratios available (139 to 312 RPM)

Best For: Speed-focused drivetrains

Cost: Medium-High

Notes: Very reliable, available in high-speed versions

NeveRest Motor

Specifications: 105 RPM, 110 oz-in stall torque

Best For: Heavy lifting, high-torque mechanisms

Cost: Higher

Notes: Most torque per motor, excellent for slide systems

REV Smart Robot Servo

Specifications: 270-degree rotation, programmable stall torque

Best For: Precise angular positioning (claws, arms)

Cost: Medium

Notes: Not for drivetrain, essential for mechanism control

Choosing Motors for Different Systems

Drivetrain

Use high-torque motors (76+ oz-in) for pushing power. Gear down for more torque, up for more speed. Tank drive typically uses 2-4 motors.

Linear Slides

Use NeveRest (110 oz-in) or geared-down HD Hex motors. Lifting mechanisms need high torque to support load without slipping.

Intake/Shooter

Use higher RPM motors (312 RPM) for fast pickup and ejection. Torque less critical for these applications.

Mechanisms

Use servos (0.5-5 oz-in) for controlled positioning. Motors for continuous rotation mechanisms.

Gearing & Speed Control

Use gearboxes to adjust motor output:

  • Higher Gear Ratio: Slower speed, more torque (e.g., 4:1)
  • Lower Gear Ratio: Higher speed, less torque (e.g., 1:1)
  • Direct Drive: No gearing, full motor RPM and torque

Example: HD Hex (312 RPM) with 4:1 gearing = 78 RPM output with 4x torque

Selection Tips

  • Start with HD Hex motors for beginners - versatile and reliable
  • Calculate load requirements before choosing motors
  • Consider space constraints - larger motors may not fit
  • Think about future upgrades when planning
  • Test motor speed/torque tradeoffs during build
  • Document your final motor configuration for reference
